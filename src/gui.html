PRISMA

how this works

light

Inside glass, every wavelength travels at its own speed — that is why a prism fans white light into a rainbow. Newton did this to sunlight in 1666. PRISMA does it to your kick drum. Same physics, different century, louder laboratory.

the curve

You are drawing a group-delay curve D(f): how many seconds each frequency arrives late, mapped logarithmically between F Min and F Max. Straight from the blackboard:

t = log(f / fmin) / log(fmax / fmin) 0..1 w = (tilt + 1) / 2 D(f) = spread · ( w · t^shape + (1−w) · (1 − t^shape) )

Yes — this is the actual curve your knobs are drawing. No marketing physics in this laboratory.

pure phase

The curve is integrated into phase, and only phase:

φ(f) = −2π · ∫₀^f D(ν) dν |H(f)| = 1 everywhere

Magnitude one, at every frequency, always. PRISMA never touches your levels — no EQ, no resonance, no secret sauce. Only time. Every frequency still arrives — just fashionably late.

the engine

That spectrum becomes an exact FIR kernel (up to 2 seconds of pure delay-per-frequency), computed on a worker thread so the audio path never waits for math. Playback runs through uniform partitioned convolution — overlap-save, FFT, the classics. Turn a knob and the new kernel slides in through two parallel convolvers with a 2048-sample crossfade: no clicks, no zipper, no drama. The fixed 512-sample block is reported to your DAW as latency, so your timeline never finds out.

the knobs

spread — how wide the temporal fan opens. Seconds of dispersion; the size of your rainbow.
tilt — who gets to be late: +1 the highs (rising chirp), −1 the lows (falling drop), and every diplomacy in between.
shape — the curvature of the dispersion law, from soft liquid sweeps to laser chirps.
mix — dry/wet. The dry path is delay-compensated, because fairness.
